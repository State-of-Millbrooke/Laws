*Chapter 5, Statutes of Millbrooke*

## INTERPRETATION AND APPLICATION

#### Interpretation

1.	In general, the elements, principles, standards of proof, defences, remedies, and procedures applicable in the law of torts, contracts, and property in a common law jurisdiction may be applied in the courts of Millbrooke, unless it is demonstrably impractical to do so.
2.	If this Act conflicts with any common law elements, principles, standards of proof, defences, remedies, or procedures, this Act modifies the common law to the extent of that conflict.

#### Cause of action

3.	A tort provided in this Act constitutes a cause of action for a civil claim.
4.	This Act does not affect the right of a person to bring a civil claim for a cause of action provided in any other Act.
5.	This Act does not affect the right of a person to bring a civil claim for a cause of action ordinarily available in a common law jurisdiction.

#### Jurisdiction

6.	The District Court is a court of competent jurisdiction to hear and render judgment in a civil claim brought for a cause of action under this Act.
7.	The District Court is a court of competent jurisdiction to hear and render judgment in a civil claim brought for a cause of action under any other Act, unless that other Act otherwise provides.

## CAUSES OF ACTION

### PART 1 – INTENTIONAL TORTS – INDIVIDUALS – DIRECT  

#### Battery

8.	It is a tort to, intentionally and as a direct consequence of an action, inflict harmful, injurious, or offensive contact on another person.

#### Assault

9.	It is a tort to, intentionally and as a direct consequence of an action, create a reasonably plausible and imminent apprehension of harmful, injurious, or offensive contact on another person.

#### Wrongful death 

10.	It is a tort to, intentionally and as a direct consequence of an action, cause the death of another person.

#### False imprisonment

11.	It is a tort to, intentionally and as a direct consequence of an action or omission, cause another person to be physically restrained in a place.

-	A person is physically restrained when no reasonable option is available for the person to leave a specific place.

12.	It is a tort to, intentionally and as a direct consequence of an action or omission, cause another person to be psychologically restrained in a place.

-	A person is psychologically restrained where the person is subject to psychological pressure such that a reasonable person would not have been able to use the available options to leave a specific place.

### PART 2 – INTENTIONAL TORTS – INDIVIDUALS – INDIRECT  

#### Invasion of privacy

13.	It is a tort to intentionally do any reprehensible action to interfere or intrude without justification into the privacy or personal affairs of another person.

#### Public disclosure of private facts

14.	It is a tort to intentionally disclose information that is embarrassing and private about another person without their consent or justification.

#### Justifications
15.	A tort in part 2 may be justified where a person is authorized by a law, regulation, or court order to commit the tort.
16.	A tort in part 2 may be justified by the freedom of expression only if the public has a clear interest in knowing the information, and mere sensationalism is not a justification.

### PART 3 – INTENTIONAL TORTS – PROPERTY

#### Trespass to land

17.	It is a tort to, as a direct consequence of an action, make unauthorized entry onto the real property of another by making contact with the property through the person’s body or through an object that the person has set in motion.

-	A person only has a cause of action for this tort if, at the time of contact, the person has the right to possession of the real property, either as the owner or as a possessor.

#### Trespass to chattels 

18.	It is a tort to, as a direct consequence of an action, make unauthorized contact with the personal property of another and cause damage, however minimal, to the personal property.

-	A person only has a cause of action for this tort if, at the time of contact, the person has the right to possession of the personal property or the right to its exclusive control.

#### Conversion

19.	It is a tort to take possession of the chattel of another person with the intention to deny the property rights of the possessor or to deprive the possessor of their rights as a possessor.
20.	It is a tort to destroy, cede, sell, or transfer the chattel of another person.

#### Detinue

21.	It is a tort to remain in possession of the chattel of another person, once the person having the right to possession of the chattel has made an unequivocal request for the return of the chattel, for a longer period of time than is reasonably necessary to return the chattel, or to have lost it as a result of one’s own intentional or negligent actions.

-	A person only has a cause of action for this tort if the person has the right to possession of the chattel or the right to its exclusive control.

### PART 4 – ECONOMIC INTERESTS

#### Fraud

22.	It is a tort to make a false declaration with some level of knowledge of the falsehood of the representation, whether through knowledge or recklessness, to induce another person to rely on the representation in such a way as to cause injury, loss, or damages.

#### Inducing breach of contract

23.	It is a tort to, knowing that a valid and enforceable contract exists, intentionally induce or procure a breach of the contract in such a way as to cause injury, loss, or damages to a party to the contract. 

####  Unlawful interference with economic relations

24.	It is a tort to do any unlawful act against a third party with the intention to, and which does, cause injury, loss, or damages to another person.

-	An unlawful act is any act which could give rise to a civil cause of action in tort or in contract by the third party, or any criminal act or omission.

#### Intimidation

25.	It is a tort to threaten another person or a third party with an unlawful act, with the intention to cause injury, loss, or damages to the other person.

-	An unlawful act is any act which could give rise to a civil cause of action in tort or in contract by the third party, or any criminal act or omission.

### PART 5 – NEGLIGENCE

26.	It is a tort for any person who owes a duty of care to another person to breach that duty of care and as a result, cause injury, loss, or damages to the other person, where the person’s actions are the factual cause and proximate cause of the damages.

### PART 6 – DEFAMATION

27.	It is a tort to publish any publications, actions, or words that refer to another person and that tend to diminish or degrade the other person in the esteem of reasonable people in society, or to expose the other person to hatred, contempt, or ridicule.

-	To publish means to communicate to a third party—a party other than the person to which the publications, actions, or words refer.
-	It is not necessary for a person to be the author of a publication, action, or word to commit this tort; each person who participates in the publication may be liable.

### PART 7 – BREACH OF CONTRACT

28.	Any person who breaches a valid and enforceable contract is liable in a civil action in respect of that breach.

### PART 8 – UNJUST ENRICHMENT

29.	Any person who receives a tangible benefit or advantage is liable in a civil action to any other person who suffers a corresponding loss or disadvantage if there is no legal, contractual, or lawful justification for the person receiving the tangible benefit or advantage.

-	A tangible benefit includes money, property, or uncompensated labour or services.

### PART 9 – ABUSE OF PROCESS

30.	Any person who intentionally abuses a legal, regulatory, administrative, or other process in such a way as to cause injury, loss, or damages to another person is liable in a civil action to the other person.

- It is notably an abuse of process to:
  - make a frivolous or fraudulent complaint;
  - make a false statement that accuses some other person of having done an unlawful act, some wrongdoing, or a criminal act;
  - do anything intended to cause another person to be suspected of having done an unlawful act, some wrongdoing, or a criminal act the other person has not done, or to divert suspicion from himself or herself;
  - reporting that an unlawful act, some wrongdoing, or a criminal act has been done when it has not been done; or
  - reporting or in any other way making it known or causing it to be made known that he or she or some other person had been victim to an unlawful act, some wrongdoing, or a criminal act when he or she or that other person had not been such a victim.

### PART 10 – CLAIMS AGAINST THE GOVERNMENT

#### Immunity

31.	In this part, the government includes:

-	A department, agency, or institution created by the [Constitution]( https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/tree/main/Constitution), a law, or a regulation;
-	A department, department head, or employee of the public service, within the meaning of the [*Public Service Employment Act*]( https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/blob/main/Public%20Laws/%5BS-2%5D%20Public%20Service%20Employment%20Act.md); and
-	A peace officer, within the meaning of the [*State Code*](https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/tree/main/State%20Code).

32.	Except as provided in this or another Act, no civil claim may be commenced or maintained against the government because of anything done or omitted by the government in the performance or intended performance of its duties or in the exercise or intended exercise of any power in relation to its duties.

#### Misfeasance in public office

33.	It is a tort for a department head, employee of the public service, or peace officer to negligently or intentionally do any action that is unlawful, prohibited, criminal, or unauthorized in the performance or of his or her duties or in the exercise of any power in relation to his or her duties, in such a way as to cause injury, loss, or damages to another person.
34.	In this part:

-	An action is unlawful, prohibited, or unauthorized if it contravenes a statute, policy, regulation, order, directive, court order, or the [Constitution]( https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/tree/main/State%20Code); and
-	An action is criminal if it contravenes the [*State Code*]( https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/tree/main/State%20Code).

35.	In a civil claim for misfeasance in public office, the person with the claim must name one or more individuals against whom the claim is made, and must not name the claim against a department, agency, or institution of the government.
36.	In a civil claim for misfeasance in public office, the only remedy is punitive damages up to $4000.
37.	In a civil claim for misfeasance in public office, the individuals against whom the claim is made are personally and severally liable to pay for any monetary damages a court may order.

#### Improper administration

38.	It is a tort for the government to negligently or intentionally make an operational decision in such a way as to cause injury, loss, or damages to another person.
39.	It is a tort for the government to make, administer, implement, promulgate, maintain, or put into practice a decision, policy, regulation, order, directive, procedure, or practice that contravenes a statute or the [Constitution]( https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/tree/main/State%20Code), in such a way as to cause injury, loss, or damages to another person.
40.	In a civil claim for improper administration, the only remedy is an injunction:

-	setting aside the decision, policy, regulation, order, directive, procedure, or practice; 
-	restraining the government from making, administering, implementing, promulgating, maintaining, or putting into practice the decision, policy, regulation, order, directive, procedure, or practice;
-	directing the government to remedy the injury, loss, or damages so caused.

#### Mode of claims under other Acts

41.	In a civil claim against the government brought for a cause of action under the [*Public Service Employment Act*](https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/blob/main/Public%20Laws/%5BS-2%5D%20Public%20Service%20Employment%20Act.md), the [*Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act*]( https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/blob/main/Public%20Laws/%5BS-3%5D%20Public%20Servants%20Disclosure%20Protection%20Act.md), or any other Act, unless the other Act otherwise provides, the person with the claim must name a department, agency, or institution of the government against whom the claim is made.

#### Representation by Attorney General

42.	In a civil claim against the government, only the Attorney General must represent the individual, department, agency, or institution against whom the claim is made.

#### Discretion to refuse representation 

43.	If, in the opinion of the Attorney General, a cause of action against the government is well-founded or the government is liable for a claim, the Attorney General has a discretion to refuse to represent the government in respect of that cause of action or claim.
44.	If, in the opinion of the Attorney General, an employee of the government has done anything unlawful, prohibited, criminal, or unauthorized, the Attorney General may refuse to represent the employee in respect of a cause of action or claim against the employee for that conduct.
45.	Where a civil action involves multiple causes of action or claims, the Attorney General is entitled to exercise the discretion to refuse to represent the government in respect of each cause of action or claim independently, subject to a joinder.

#### Mode of claims where representation refused

46.	Where the Attorney General refuses to represent the government in a cause of action for misfeasance in public office, a court may either:

-	grant such lawful remedies requested by the person with the claim as the court considers appropriate and just in the circumstances and dismiss the claim; or
-	on the request of an individual against whom the claim is made, allow the claim to proceed against the individual and order that the claim is no longer a claim against the government.

47.	Where the Attorney General refuses to represent the government in a cause of action or claim other than for misfeasance in public office, a court must grant such lawful remedies requested by the person with the claim as the court considers appropriate and just in the circumstances and dismiss the claim.

### PART 11 – APPLICATION TO REVIEW RECORD OF ARREST OR CITATION

#### Application

48.	Every person who has been arrested or cited by a peace officer for an offence or infraction under the [*State Code*]( https://github.com/State-of-Millbrooke/Laws/tree/main/State%20Code) may apply to the District Court for review if the person believes that he or she did not commit the offence or infraction.
49.	A person bringing an application under this part must name only the peace officer who recorded the arrest or citation as the respondent.
50.	Only one record of arrest or citation may be reviewed in each application under this part.

#### Conviction bars application

51.	A person who is arrested or cited for an offence or infraction and subsequently convicted of the offence or infraction for which he or she was arrested or cited may not make an application under this part in respect of that offence or infraction.

#### Procedure

52.	The procedure for hearing an application under this part is as follows:

-	Application is filed;
-	Court determines, based on the merits of the application, whether to grant or deny review;
-	If review is granted, the peace officer who recorded the arrest or citation submits an affidavit or brief;
-	Court determines, based on the whole of the evidence, whether to grant or deny the application.

#### Legality 

53.	A court may grant an application under this part and administer remedy if it is satisfied that the arrest or citation was unlawful, in that:

-	the peace officer did not have a warrant authorizing the arrest; and
-	the peace officer did not have reasonable and probable grounds to believe that an offence or infraction was committed, is being committed, or will be committed.

54.	The burden of proof for demonstrating the legality of an arrest or citation lay on the respondent.
55.	A court must consider the arrest or citation report by the peace officer who recorded the arrest or citation as proof for determining the legality of an arrest or citation.

#### Actual innocence

56.	A court may grant an application under this part and administer remedy if it is satisfied that the applicant did not in fact commit, including by reason that he or she is to an affirmative defence to, the offence or infraction for which he or she was arrested or cited.
57.	The burden of proof for demonstrating actual innocence lay on the applicant.

#### Consent

58.	A court may grant an application under this part and administer remedy by consent of the peace officer who recorded the arrest or citation in question.

#### Attorney General may intervene

59.	The Attorney General may intervene on behalf of the respondent in an application under this part.

## REMEDIES

60.	The remedies available in a civil claim brought for a tort in part 1 are compensatory and punitive damages up to $2000.
61.	The remedies available in a civil claim brought for a tort in parts 2 and 6 are compensatory and punitive damages up to $2000 and equitable remedies.
62.	The remedies available in a civil claim brought for a tort in parts 3, 4 and 5 are compensatory and punitive damages up to $4000 and equitable remedies.
63.	The remedies available in a civil claim for breach of contract (part 7) and unjust enrichment (part 8) are compensatory and liquidated damages and equitable remedies.
64.	The remedies available in a civil claim for abuse of process (part 9) are punitive damages up to $4000 and equitable remedies.
65.	The remedies available in a civil claim brought for a cause of action under part 10 are as prescribed in this Act.
66.	The remedy available in an application brought under part 11 is an order setting aside records of the offence or infraction.
67.	The remedies available in a civil claim brought for a cause of action under any other Act are, unless otherwise provided, such remedies as the court considers appropriate and just in the circumstances, including equitable remedies.

#### Dollar limits

68.	Where dollar limits apply to monetary damages, the dollar limit applies to the total of the monetary damages awarded in respect of each tort independently.

#### Equitable remedies

69.	Equitable remedies include interlocutory and permanent injunctions, specific performance, constructive remedies, restitution, and rescission.
70.	There is no entitlement, as of right, to equitable remedies.
71.	A court, in determining whether to grant or refuse an injunction, must notably consider whether refusing the injunction would cause harm irreparable through monetary compensation.

## MODIFICATION TO THE COMMON LAW 

#### Certain torts not a cause of action

72.	Notwithstanding anything in this Act, the following are not torts for which a cause of action may be brought, unless another Act creates an independent cause of action for that tort:

-	Appropriation of personality;
-	Discrimination;
-	Intentional or negligent infliction of mental suffering or emotional distress; and
-	Nuisance.

## PROCEDURE

#### Right to a claim

73.	A person other than a resident of the State of Millbrooke has no right to any cause of action under this or any other Act or at common law, and may not bring a civil claim or any action whatsoever in the courts of Millbrooke, except for an application under part 11.

#### *Prima facie* responsibility

74.	The person with the claim has the burden to prove their claim or that they are otherwise entitled to relief.
75.	When the elements of a tort are proved, the person against whom the claim is made is presumed to have committed the tort in question.

#### Defence

76.	A person who is presumed to have committed a tort may present a defence which, if conclusive, overturns that presumption.

#### Liability

77.	When the elements of a tort and the lack of defence have been established, the person against whom the claim is made is liable for the tort in question. 

#### Remedies

78.	A person liable for a tort is liable for any remedies a court may order. 

#### Standard of proof

79.	The standard of proof in all civil claims is a balance of probabilities.

#### Limitation period 

80.	A civil claim must be commenced within 4 months of the date on which the person with the claim first knew:

-	that the injury, loss, or damage had occurred; 
-	that the injury, loss, or damage was caused by or contributed to by an act or omission; 
-	that the act or omission was that of the person against whom the claim is made; and 
-	that, having regard to the nature of the injury, loss, or damage, a civil claim would be an appropriate means to seek to remedy it.

#### Reasonable person

81.	In general, there is always an obligation for each person to act as a reasonable person would have done in similar circumstances.
82.	The reasonable person is a person who possesses and exercises the normal and reasonable diligence and attention expected in society.
83.	The reasonable person test may be used to determine if a person’s actions or behaviors were appropriate in a given situation.
84.	The reasonable person test is objective, subject to circumstances that can change what is expected of a reasonable person, and variable where a person presents themselves as possessing particular skills.
