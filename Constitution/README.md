<h1>Constitution of the State of Millbrooke</h1>

**Enacted 7 February 2026**

## INTERPRETATION AND APPLICATION

#### Supremacy of the Constitution

1.	The Constitution is the supreme law of the State of Millbrooke, and any law, enactment, regulation, constitution, or statutory instrument that is inconsistent with the provisions of the Constitution is, to the extent of the inconsistency, of no force or effect.

#### Application of the law

2.	The laws of the State of Millbrooke apply to all Roblox experiences and Discord servers related to the State of Millbrooke and other venues as the Developers may exclusively designate from time to time.
3.	No law, enactment, regulation, constitution, or statutory instrument of any jurisdiction other than that of the State of Millbrooke, including the federal laws and constitution of the United States and the laws and constitution of any other state, is recognized, applicable, or has any force whatsoever in the State of Millbrooke except to the extent as the Developer may otherwise order. 

#### Imperative and permissive

4.	In the Constitution and in all the laws of the State of Millbrooke, the expressions “shall” and “must” is to be construed as imperative, and the expression “may” as permissive.

## BILL OF RIGHTS

#### Equality of rights and individuals

5.	Every person has the right to life, liberty and security of the person and the right not to be deprived thereof except in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice.

#### Equitable justice

6.	Every person has the right not to be arbitrarily detained or imprisoned.
7.	Every person has the right on arrest or detention:
- to be informed promptly of the reasons therefor; 
- to retain and instruct counsel without delay when being interrogated and to be informed of that right; 
- to have the validity of the detention determined by way of habeas corpus; and 
- to be released if the detention is not lawful.

8.	The privilege of the writ of habeas corpus may not be suspended except in accordance with law, and only during rebellion or invasion where the public safety may require its suspension.
9.	Every person charged with an offence has the right:
- to be informed without unreasonable delay of the specific offence; 
- to be tried within a reasonable time; 
- not to be compelled to be a witness in proceedings against that person in respect of the offence; 
- to be presumed innocent until proven guilty; and 
- to be tried in publicly by an independent and impartial tribunal.

10.	Every person convicted of an offence has the right:
- not to be subjected to any cruel and unusual treatment or punishment; 
- not be punished by death; 
- to the benefit of the lesser punishment if the punishment for the offence has been varied between the time of commission and the time of sentencing; 
- not to be tried for the same offence twice except at their own request; and 
- not to be punished for the same offence twice. 

11.	A witness who testifies in any proceedings has the right not to have any incriminating evidence so given used to incriminate that witness in any other proceedings, except in a prosecution for perjury or for the giving of contradictory evidence.

#### Searches and seizures

12.	Every person has the right to be secure against unreasonable search or seizure.
13.	Seizures of private property is prohibited without a search warrant authorized by a court, unless a peace officer has probable cause the property contains anything unlawful. 
14.	If a court concludes that evidence was obtained in a manner that infringed or denied any rights or freedoms guaranteed in the Constitution, the evidence must be excluded if it is established that, having regard to all the circumstances, the admission of it in the proceedings would bring the administration of justice into disrepute.

#### Compensation for taking private property

15.	Should private property be taken for any public use, just compensation must be made therefor.

#### Freedom of speech, of the press, and of assembly

16.	Every person has the freedom of thought, belief, opinion and expression, including freedom of the press and other media of communication. 
17.	Every person may freely speak, write, and publish his or her sentiments on all subjects, being responsible for the abuse of that right; and no law may be passed to restrain or abridge the liberty of speech or of the press. 
18.	Every person has the freedom to peacefully assemble and gather with others to express ideas or beliefs and to petition the government. 

#### Right to bear arms

19.	Every person has the right to bear arms, which may not be infringed, except through licensing and restrictions on open carry in areas regulated by local or state law.

#### Remedy

20.	Anyone whose rights or freedoms, as guaranteed by the Constitution, have been infringed or denied may apply to a court of competent jurisdiction to obtain such remedy as the court considers appropriate and just in the circumstances.

#### Scope of rights guaranteed 

21.	The guarantee in the Constitution of certain rights and freedoms may not be construed as denying the existence of any other rights or freedoms that exist.
22.	The Bill of Rights is subject to the implicit and explicit limits contained in the Constitution and may not be construed as to abrogate any other section of the Constitution.
23.	The rights guaranteed can be subject only to such reasonable limits prescribed by law as can be demonstrably justified in the context of a virtual simulation.

## FRAMEWORK OF GOVERNMENTS

#### Separation

24.	The Constitution must be interpreted to give effect to the separation of the branches of government, where each branch is completely independent of one another, and where a system of checks and balances is strictly applied to each branch to moderate and control the power of the other two branches. 
25.	Unless prescribed by law, no branch of government has power over any other branch or branches of government. 

#### Other orders of government

26.	The Developers may exclusively incorporate, decorporate, modify, amalgamate, and abolish other orders of government and local and regional government structures.
27.	The Developers may exclusively devise laws respecting the structure of local government and municipal charters.
28.	Local government laws and municipal charters may empower local councils to enact by-laws and ordinances respecting matters of a local nature and to conduct local elections for local councils.

## THE ASSEMBLY

#### Composition

29.	The whole of the membership of the Assembly consists of 8 members.
30.	Members of the Assembly are elected in a general election to a term of 6 months.

#### Powers and privileges

31.	The Assembly is empowered to make laws in and for the State of Millbrooke, to scrutinize statutory nominations, to examine the performance and fitness of government officials, to examine witnesses in relation to a function of the government, and to hold the government to account.
32.	The Assembly is not competent to conduct business, except to adjourn, unless at least one half of the whole of the membership of the Assembly, including the Presiding Officer, are present.
33.	The Assembly may determine its own rules of procedure and standing orders to be followed in the conduct of its business; and must conduct business in accordance with said rules and orders adopted by resolution. 
34.	The Assembly is entitled to make orders providing for its governance.
35.	The Assembly may establish committees as it sees fit and refer any matter to a committee for report and delegate, with or without restrictions or conditions, to any committee any of the executive or administrative powers exercisable by the Assembly.
36.	The Assembly may establish and equip departments, agencies, and offices as it may from time to time deem expedient in the exercise of its powers and delegate to departments, agencies, and offices any of the executive or administrative powers exercisable by the Assembly.
37.	The Assembly has the duty, in all instances while conducting business and exercising its authority, to act in the public interest.

#### Presiding Officers

38.	The Speaker is the Presiding Officer of the Assembly.
39.	At the first sitting of the Assembly and at subsequent sittings where the office of Speaker pro tempore is vacant, the Assembly must elect one of its members to be Speaker pro tempore before proceeding to business.
40.	An election of Speaker pro tempore may be presided by the Speaker or a person designated by the Speaker.
41.	Each member of the Assembly not including the person presiding is qualified to nominate themselves for the office of Speaker pro tempore.
42.	Each member of the Assembly not including the person presiding is entitled to cast up to the number of candidates for Speaker pro tempore of votes, but no votes may be cast for the same candidate.
43.	The Speaker pro tempore may be called to be the Presiding Officer of the Assembly, except when the Speaker is present.
44.	The Presiding Officer must act as an impartial and neutral presiding officer during the sitting for which he or she is the Presiding Officer.
45.	The Presiding Officer must maintain order and decorum, remain neutral, make rulings on points of order, and direct the proceedings of the Assembly during the sitting for which he or she is the Presiding Officer. 
46.	The Presiding Officer must not vote on a question before the Assembly or participate in debate during the sitting for which he or she is the Presiding Officer
47.	The Presiding Officer is entitled to a casting vote when the Assembly is equally divided on a question.

#### Legislative business

48.	The Assembly may adopt motions, resolutions, and bills and approve statutory nominations, provided more than one-half of the members present vote in the affirmative in respect of the question.
49.	The Assembly may move to conduct business in committee, initiate investigations, conduct hearings, subpoena witnesses, testimony, and documents, and issue reports and recommendations, provided more than one-half of the members present vote in the affirmative in respect of the question.
50.	Committees duly established may conduct business to the extent authorized by its mandate, provided more than one-half of the members present in committee vote in the affirmative in respect of the question.
51.	The Assembly may adopt a bill to amend the Constitution, a bill notwithstanding the Governor’s objection, and articles of impeachment, provided more than one-half of the whole of the membership of the Assembly vote in the affirmative in respect of the question.
52.	At all sittings of the Assembly, each member of the Assembly is entitled to cast one vote in the affirmative, in the negative, or in abstention in respect of each question put to a vote at that sitting, subject to the restrictions on the Presiding Officer voting.
53.	For the purpose of tabulating votes, a vote in abstention has the same effect as if the member so abstaining was not present at the sitting in respect of that question.
54.	If the votes for and against a question are equal or otherwise not sufficient, the question is defeated.
55.	The Assembly must keep the doors of its sittings open except when the public welfare may require secrecy, provided more than one-half of the members present vote in the affirmative to close the sitting to the public; and the Assembly must not vote on the adoption of a bill at a sitting closed to the public.
56.	The Assembly must keep a journal of its sittings and of the votes of its members in all businesses it conducts and publish that journal to the public.

#### Effect of legislative acts

57.	Where the Assembly adopts a bill, except a bill adopted notwithstanding the Governor’s objection, that bill becomes law when the Governor communicates to the Chief Electoral Officer that he or she has agreed to the bill in the form that it was adopted; and if no such communication is made, that bill becomes law 72 hours after its adoption.
58.	If, within 72 hours of the Assembly adopting a bill, except a bill adopted notwithstanding the Governor’s objection, the Governor communicates his or her objection to that bill to the Chief Electoral Officer, that bill does not become law.
59.	Where the Assembly adopts a bill notwithstanding the Governor’s objection, that bill becomes law immediately.
60.	Where the Assembly approves a statutory nomination, the nominee is eligible to take oath and then enter office immediately.
61.	Where the Assembly adopts articles of impeachment, the Supreme Court is seized of those articles immediately. 
62.	Where the Assembly adopts a bill to amend the Constitution, the bill shall be put to a referendum by the Chief Electoral Officer without delay.
63.	Where the Assembly adopts a motion or resolution, that motion or resolution takes effect immediately. 
64.	Where a legislative committee adopts a measure, that measure takes effect immediately.

## THE EXECUTIVE

#### The Governor

65.	The Governor is elected in a general election to a term of 6 months.
66.	The Governor is responsible for peace, order, and good government.
67.	The Governor may delegate and withdraw any of the executive or administrative powers exercisable by the Governor to and from the Lieutenant Governor.
68.	The Governor has the duty, in all instances while conducting business and exercising his or her authority, to act in the public interest.

#### The Lieutenant Governor

69.	The Lieutenant Governor is elected in the same general election as the Governor.
70.	The Lieutenant Governor may exercise any powers delegated to him or her by the Governor.
71.	The Lieutenant Governor is Speaker of the Assembly.
72.	The Lieutenant Governor has the duty, in all instances while conducting business and exercising his or her authority, to act in the public interest.

#### Office of the Governor

73.	The Governor may determine the organization and operations of the executive department and the executive offices known collectively as the Office of the Governor, including:
- Establishing, varying, and dissolving executive offices or subsidiary government bodies under the Office of the Governor;
- Prescribing the powers and duties of executive offices;
- Determining policies for executive offices;
- Giving direction to persons appointed to an executive office;
- Appointing and dismissing persons to and from executive offices; and
- Delegating and withdrawing any of the executive or administrative powers exercisable by the Governor to and from a person appointed by the Governor, except the power with respect to bills, executive orders, and appointments and nominations.

#### Executive orders

74.	The Governor may issue executive orders, insofar as they are consistent with the law, to:
- Prescribe powers and duties to departments, agencies, and commissions;
- Determine policies for departments, agencies, and commissions as necessary;
- Determine the department, agency, or commission that will exercise any of the duties or functions under an enactment;
- Give direction to a department, agency, or commission and to any persons appointed in a department, agency, or commission;
- Enact regulations as secondary legislation when empowered to do so by a statute; and
- Exercise other executive powers when permissible or commanded by a statute.

#### Bills

75.	The Governor may communicate to the Chief Electoral Officer that he or she agrees with a bill adopted by the Assembly, in the form that it was adopted, to the to cause that bill to become law as of the time of the communication.
76.	The Governor may communicate to the Chief Electoral Officer that he or she objects to a bill adopted by the Assembly, in the form that it was adopted; and if that communication is made within 72 hours of the Assembly adopting that bill, that bill does not become law.
77.	Once the Chief Electoral Officer receives the Governor’s communication as to his or her agreement or objection with respect to a bill, that agreement or objection is final and irrevocable.
78.	The Governor may not do anything to cause only a portion of a bill to become law.

#### Appointments and nominations

79.	The Governor may nominate persons who appear fit to the Governor for approval by the Assembly to fill a vacancy in the following positions:
- the department head of any department;
- the judge or justice of any court; and
- all other positions created by statute for which a nomination is the procedure for appointment established by law;
- all other positions created by statute whose appointment is not otherwise provided for.

80.	The Governor may appoint a person who appears fit to the Governor to fill a vacancy in a position which would otherwise require a nomination only if that vacancy occurs while the Assembly is dissolved; in which case the appointment expires at the end of the Assembly’s next sitting.

#### Diplomats; ambassadors

81.	The Governor is the chief diplomat and may, with the consent of the Assembly, bind the State of Millbrooke to treaties.
82.	The Governor exclusively may appoint and revoke the appointment of ambassadors and diplomats.
83.	The Governor may communicate with other states, foreign powers and accredit, remove, receive, or expel their ambassadors and diplomats.

#### Miscellaneous

84.	The Governor may grant pardons to set aside criminal offences and commute fines and sentences, except for convictions by a court.

## THE JUDICIARY

#### Composition; eligibility 

85.	The judicial power is vested in the courts of the State of Millbrooke, and extends to all cases, in law and equity, arising under the Constitution and the laws of the State of Millbrooke.
86.	No court may be created, abolished, or modified other than by amending the Constitution.
87.	The Supreme Court consists of up to 4 puisne justices and the Chief Justice.
88.	The District Court consists of up to 10 district judges.
89.	A judge or justice of any court is nominated by the Governor and holds office for life or until resignation or otherwise removed from office.
90.	To be eligible to be hold office as a judge or justice of any court, a person
- must be a resident of the State of Millbrooke, 
- must not be convicted of an offence, and
- must not hold any other public office or elected office except when explicitly permitted to do so by a statute or in cases of ex officio seats.

91.	Despite anything in the Constitution, a judge or justice may sit as an impartial arbiter on a commission, board, or tribunal that serves a public function.

#### Powers

92.	Any judge or justice of a court may exercise the powers of that court unless a power is explicitly prescribed as being exercisable only by the judges or justices of that court sitting together. 
93.	A court may set aside criminal records of any person. 
94.	A court may issue warrants for the arrest of persons and search of property.
95.	A court may preside over the trial of a person charged with an offence for which, on conviction, the person is liable to be sentenced to a term of imprisonment.

96.	A court may make an order finding a person to be in contempt and may impose a fine on or imprison the person for contempt if the person has been served with a court order or summons and fails or refuses to do any of the following: 
- attend before the court,
- take an oath or make an affirmation,
- answer questions, and
- produce records or things in the person's possession or control.

#### Chief Justice; Chief Judge

97.	The Chief Justice is responsible for the following:
- the general superintendence and administration of the courts, 
- maintaining the discipline of the judges and justices, 
- determining rules of procedure for the Supreme Court and for the District Court, 
- maintaining the functions of the courts, and
- may employ the necessary staff to exercise these duties and powers.

98.	The Chief Justice may designate one or more judges of the District Court as Chief Judge and designate to him or her any of the responsibilities of the Chief Justice in respect of the District Court and its judges.

#### Supreme Court

99.	The Supreme Court sitting together may deliberate on and adopt motions and policies relevant to and within the scope of the proper administration of the courts; and those motions and policies are deemed to be valid and have the same effect as a regulation.
100.	The Supreme Court sitting together has the jurisdiction to hear all appeals from the District Court and to render judgment in the same and may set aside any judgment of the District Court.
101.	The Supreme Court has the discretion to determine which appeals to hear, except where the judgment under appeal is a conviction for a misdemeanour or felony offence, and this appeal must be heard as of right.
102.	The Supreme Court is the court of final appeal, and no judgment of that court may be considered for appeal, except pursuant to a resolution of the Developers.
103.	The Supreme Court sitting together has the jurisdiction to try articles of impeachment and to render judgment in the same, in the same manner of a judgment in an ordinary action, except that the only sentence upon conviction is removal from office and disqualification from public office.
104.	The Supreme Court sitting together has the jurisdiction to hear reference questions and answer the same by pronouncing on the constitutional validity of all or part of the Acts being referred; and a reference judgment has the same force and effect as a judgment in an ordinary action.

#### District Court

105.	The District Court has the jurisdiction to hear and render judgment in all civil and criminal matters and in all applications, including for judicial review.
106.	The District Court has the jurisdiction to hear and render judgment in all matter arising out of the administrative responsibilities of government, including appeals of judgments of an administrative body, adjudicator, board, tribunal, and departmental decision-maker, to the extent permitted by law. 
107.	The District Court has the jurisdiction to hear and render judgment in all matter it may be required to under law, and to pronounce on whether it is within the jurisdiction of the court to hear a matter.

## ATTORNEY GENERAL

#### Entrenched

108.	It is recognized and declared that, given the integral role of the Attorney General, the position may not be abolished other than by amending the Constitution, but its duties and powers may be further prescribed by law.

#### Attorney General

109.	The Attorney General is the legal advisor of the State of Millbrooke and to the Governor, the Assembly, and department heads in official matters.
110.	The Attorney General must represent the State of Millbrooke in all civil suits where the state is a party.
111.	The Attorney General must be granted intervener status in civil suits where the state is not a party, but where the Attorney General believes the suit may affect or implicate the state. 
112.	The Attorney General is the prosecutor-general for the State of Millbrooke and may, in the name of the State of Millbrooke, investigate and prosecute persons it believes to be committing crimes before a court.
113.	The Attorney General shall have general oversight over all government departments and agencies to ensure ethical and lawful practices are maintained.
114.	The Attorney General may issue orders and directives to any government department or agency to the extent permitted by law; and all orders and directives are subject to judicial review. 
115.	The Attorney General may initiate an inquiry into any government department or agencies or persons he or she believes have been involved in unethical or unlawful practices.
116.	The Attorney General may employ the necessary staff to exercise his or her duties and powers, together constituting the Department of Justice.
117.	The Attorney General may delegate but not relinquish any of his or her duties and powers to persons employed in the Department of Justice.
118.	The Attorney General may, should he or she find it advisable, seek a reference opinion from the Supreme Court as to the constitutional validity of all or part of an Act by referring the question to the Supreme Court for hearing and consideration.
119.	The Attorney General has the duty, in all instances while conducting business and exercising his or her authority, to act in the public interest.

## NATIONAL GUARD 

#### Entrenched

120.	It is recognized and declared that, given the integral role of the National Guard, it may not be abolished other than by amending the Constitution, but its duties and powers may be further prescribed by law.

#### Function

121.	The National Guard is the organized militia of the State of Millbrooke.
122.	The National Guard has the duty to maintain the peace and order and defend the State of Millbrooke against enemies both foreign and domestic.
123.	The National Guard is not authorized to exercise its functions outside of the State of Millbrooke.
124.	The Assembly may prescribe such functions as it may deem expedient in the maintenance of peace, order, and good government to be performed by the National Guard.

#### Commander-in-Chief

125.	The Governor is the Commander-in-Chief and may exercise due civilian authority and control of the National Guard.

#### Adjutant General

126.	The Governor may nominate a person who appears fit to the Governor for approval by the Assembly for the position of Adjutant General.
127.	The Adjutant General is the highest-ranking active-service general officer and department head of the National Guard.
128.	The Adjutant General is responsible for the following:
- maintaining the readiness of the National Guard;
- organizing and conducting trainings, operations, and exercises for the National Guard;
- the general superintendence of all activities conducted by the National Guard; and
- maintaining the discipline of the National Guard and administering military justice, pursuant to law.

129.	The Adjutant General may delegate any of any of his or her duties and powers to any member of the National Guard.

#### Activation

130.	An activation of the National Guard means to call into active service all or some of the members of the National Guard.
131.	A member of the National Guard who is lawfully called into active service must present himself or herself at the time and place designated by a superior officer or other person having lawful authority to give directions to the member.
132.	The Governor may activate the National Guard:
- to suppress insurrection, rebellion, or invasion, whether real or apprehended;
- to suppress riot;
- to provide assistance in times of crisis, disaster, or catastrophe; or
- in a manner provided by law.

#### Judge Advocate General

133.	The Governor may nominate a person who appears fit to the Governor for approval by the Assembly for the position of Judge Advocate General.
134.	The Judge Advocate General is responsible to the Assembly in the performance of the Judge Advocate General’s duties and functions.
135.	The Judge Advocate General acts as legal adviser to the Governor, the Adjutant General, and the National Guard in matters relating to military law.
136.	The Judge Advocate General has the superintendence of the administration of military justice in the National Guard.
137.	The Judge Advocate General shall conduct regular reviews of the administration of military justice.
138.	The Judge Advocate General holds a rank that is not less than lieutenant-colonel.

#### Military court

139.	Where the Judge Advocate General is of the opinion that the administration of military justice requires a military court to adjudicate anything, including to determine the guilt or innocence of a member of the National Guard in respect of service offence, the Judge Advocate General may, in consultation with the Chief Justice, appoint a judge or justice of any court to sit as a military judge and to render decisions for the military court in that particular instance.
140.	An appeal lies to the Supreme Court from a final order of the military court.

## IMMUNITY

141.	No legal proceeding for damages lies or may be commenced or maintained against a court, a judge of a court, a person acting on behalf of or under the direction of the court, or a member of the Assembly because of anything done or omitted 
- in the performance or intended performance of his or her duty, or 
- in the exercise or intended exercise of any power in relation to his or her duty. 

142.	No immunity applies to any person referred to in the preceding section in relation to anything done or omitted by that person in bad faith.

## ELECTIONS

#### Chief Electoral Officer

143.	The Developers may appoint a Chief Electoral Officer; unless otherwise ordered, the appointment is for life.
144.	Every Developer may exercise the duties and powers of the Chief Electoral Officer.
145.	The Chief Electoral Officer is responsible for the administration and regulation of all elections, recall petitions, and referendums.
146.	The Chief Electoral Officer may, as he or she may from time to time deem expedient in the exercise of his or her powers, delegate to a judge or justice any of the administrative powers exercisable by the Chief Electoral Officer.
147.	The Chief Electoral Officer may enact binding Electoral Regulations to the extent necessary to exercise his or her duties and powers. 
148.	The Electoral Regulations retain supralegislative status, and any law which is in any respect repugnant to the Electoral Regulations is of no force of effect.
149.	The Chief Electoral Officer may regulate candidate eligibility, consistent with law. 
150.	The Chief Electoral Officer may issue a writ of election when necessary to fill a vacancy in a position by election.

#### Election procedures

151.	Every resident of the State of Millbrooke is an elector and is entitled to vote in any election administered in the State of Millbrooke. 
152.	Every elector who is 13 years of age or older is eligible to submit their candidacy in any election, provided they are not convicted of a crime, disqualified from public office, or disqualified by the Chief Electoral Officer. 
153.	A writ of election initiates an electoral event; the writ must include all of the following:
- the office up for election, 
- the number of seats up for election,
- any candidate eligibility requirements,
- a medium to submit candidacy, 
- the schedule for the electoral event, and
- and any other instructions relevant to the electoral event. 

154.	The Chief Electoral Officer must allow sufficient time for nominations, campaigning, and voting to take occur, being, at a minimum:
- in general elections, 48 hours each; and 
- in special elections, 24 hours each. 

155.	All voting must be conducted through an in-game system under the oversight of a Developer, unless a Developer otherwise orders. 
156.	Voting secrecy must be preserved for all elections. 
157.	A certification of voting results concludes an electoral event; the certification of voting results must include all of the following:
- the number of votes received by each candidate,
- the candidates elected, and
- the term of office for which the candidates were elected.

158.	All voting results must be certified by a Developer or the Chief Electoral Officer before the candidates elected may take a valid oath and enter office.

#### Election schedules

159.	The Chief Electoral Officer must maintain an elections schedule pursuant to the Constitution, to be generally followed except in exigent circumstances, where changes only as much as necessary may be permitted. 
160.	A general election must be held to elect the following officials:
- the members of the Assembly, no later than 6 months following the previous general election for that office;
- the Governor, no later than 6 months following the previous general election for that office; 
- the Attorney General, no later than 4 months following the previous general election for that office; and
- any other elected official, at the interval prescribed by law.

161.	A writ of election by the Chief Electoral Officer may initiate a special election if there is a vacancy in any elected office; and a special election must be initiated if the vacancy is the result of a successful recall petition.
162.	When a writ of election for a general election has been issued for the seats in the Assembly, the Assembly stands dissolved and every member of the Assembly, including the Speaker, ceases to hold office.
163.	When a writ of election for a general election has been issued in respect of an office other than the seats in the Assembly, the office up for election is not vacated.

#### Recall procedures

164.	Every elected official is subject to recall.
165.	Every elector is eligible to submit and vote on petitions for the recall of any elected official who is subject to recall, provided they are not convicted of a crime, disqualified from public office, or disqualified by the Chief Electoral Officer.
166.	The permissible grounds for recall are: 
- misconduct, 
- failure in the execution of his or her office, 
- conduct incompatible with the due execution of his or her office, and 
- loss of confidence of the electors.

167.	A petition for recall must be submitted to the Chief Electoral Officer and he or she must approve the petition if:
- the petition lists permissible grounds for recall, 
- the official sought to be recalled has been in office for at least 10 days and has not been subject to an approved petition for recall in the previous 10 days, 
- the petition for recall explains in particular its reasons for seeking recall, 
- the petition complies with the form in which the Chief Electoral Officer requires a petition for recall to be submitted, and
- the petition appears genuine.

168.	The Chief Electoral Officer must, upon approving a petition for recall, publish the petition and begin collecting votes of “approve” and “not approve” as to the question “Do you approve or do you not approve of the petition for recall of (name of official)?” from the electors over a period not less than 48 hours. 
169.	If more electors vote “approve” than "not approve", a recall election is triggered, and the Chief Electoral Officer must issue a writ of election to that effect. 
170.	If the required threshold of votes is not met, the petition must be dismissed by the Chief Electoral Officer. 
171.	The election procedure referred to in the Constitution applies to recall elections as if it were a special election, except that the official being recalled must be included on the ballot automatically and may not be disqualified from standing for election, except for breaching the electoral regulations.

#### Referendum procedures

172.	Every elector is eligible to submit and vote on referendums on matters of public interest, including to put in force a law, provided they are not convicted of a crime, disqualified from public office, or disqualified by the Chief Electoral Officer. 
173.	A referendum must be submitted to the Chief Electoral Officer and he or she must approve the referendum if 
- the referendum is signed by at least one member of the Assembly, 
- the referendum relates to a matter of public interest 
- the referendum complies with the form in which the Chief Electoral Officer requires a referendum to be submitted, and 
- the referendum is signed by at least 5 other eligible electors.

174.	In the case of a referendum to put in force a law, the proposed bill or regulation must be submitted to the Chief Electoral Officer at the same time as the referendum.
175.	The Chief Electoral Officer must, upon approving a referendum, publish the referendum and begin collecting votes of “approve” and “not approve” as to the question “Do you approve or do you not approve of the referendum to (short description)?” from the electors over a period not less than 48 hours. 
176.	If more electors vote “approve” than "not approve", the Chief Electoral Officer must declare that the referendum is successful; and in the case of a referendum to put in force a law, declare that the proposed bill or regulation has become law and publish the law in the manner any ordinary law is published.
177.	If the required threshold of votes is not met, the Chief Electoral Officer must declare that the referendum is defeated.
178.	For greater certainty, a law put in force by a successful referendum does not require the Governor’s agreement.
179.	For greater certainty, a law put in force by a successful referendum may be amended and repealed by the Assembly in the same manner any ordinary law may be amended and repealed.

#### Breaking ties

180.	A tie is when two or more candidates have an equal number of votes in any election. 
181.	At the discretion of the Chief Electoral Officer, a tie may be broken in the following ways:
- drawing lots, a random draw performed by the Chief Electoral Officer which must be recorded and made available to the public for inspection following the draw; or
- runoff election, where all unsuccessful candidates from the original election may run in a second election.

## PROCEDURE FOR AMENDING THE CONSTITUTION

182.	If the Assembly adopts a bill to amend the Constitution, the Chief Electoral Officer must put the constitutional amendment to a referendum to seek the consent of the electors.
183.	The provisions in the Constitution respecting referendums apply to a referendum on a constitutional amendment.
184.	If a referendum on a constitutional amendment is successful, the constitutional amendment becomes law immediately.

## OATHS OF OFFICE

185.	Any judge or justice may administer an oath of office.
186.	A solemn affirmation may be taken in lieu of an oath by any person required to take an oath.
187.	The Governor, the Lieutenant Governor, the Attorney General, a member of the Assembly, and a department head must not enter office until he or she has taken and subscribed the following oath:

```
I, A.B., swear or solemnly affirm that I am qualified to hold the office of __. 
I will faithfully perform the duties of my office to the best of my ability and will not allow any private interest to influence my conduct in public matters. 
I understand and accept the responsibility entrusted to me to exercise my functions with fairness, transparency, and respect for all persons.
```

188.	A judge or justice must not enter office until he or she has taken and subscribed the following oath:

```
I am qualified to sit on the District Court/Supreme Courts to which I have been appointed. 
I will faithfully perform the duties of my office to the best of my ability and will not allow any private interest to influence my conduct in public matters. 
I will impartially administer justice irrespective of the persons before me. 
I understand and accept the responsibility entrusted to me to exercise my functions with fairness, transparency, and respect for all persons.
```

189.	The Assembly may prescribe additional oaths to be taken by persons exercising public functions before they may enter office.

## PROHIBITED OFFICES AND CONDUCT

190.	The Governor, a member of the Assembly, a department head, or an elected official is not eligible to be a judge or justice.
191.	A judge or justice is not eligible to be the Governor, a member of the Assembly, a department head, or an elected official
192.	The Governor, a member of the Assembly, a department head, a judge or justice, and any person holding public office, whether appointed or elected,
- must not allow any private interest to influence his or her conduct in public matters;
- must disclose any direct or indirect conflict of interest he or she has in all matter; and 
- must not participate in the discussion, vote, or adjudication of any matters in which he or she has a direct or indirect conflict of interest.

193.	If a person disqualified from public office is elected, the person's election is void.

## IMPEACHMENT

194.	The following officials are subject to impeachment:
- a member of the Assembly,
- the Governor,
- the Lieutenant Governor
- the Attorney General,
- a person who occupies a position nominated by the Governor,
- a judge or justice of any court, 
- a person appointed to a position by the Developer, and
- a person whose position is prescribed by law as being subject to impeachment.

195.	The Assembly may adopt articles of impeachment against an official subject to impeachment who, in its opinion:
- is inactive,
- has been convicted of a felony or misdemeanour offence under the _State Code_,
- exhibits corruption or malfeasance,
- neglects his or her duties, or
- conducts himself or herself in a manner unbecoming of his or her office.

196.	An official who is impeached must be served with all the articles of impeachment adopted against him and be afforded a trial before the Supreme Court.
197.	An official who is impeached is suspended from his or her functions immediately, but his or her office is not vacated until that official has been tried and convicted.
198.	Any official who is subject to impeachment may not be removed from office except through impeachment and only on conviction on an article of impeachment, in addition only to other means of removing an official from office provided in the Constitution.

## RESIGNATION AND ABSENCE

199.	An elected official who wishes to resign shall declare his or her intention to resign to the Chief Electoral Officer.
200.	The Chief Electoral Officer must issue a writ of election for a special election in all cases where an elected official resigns, except if a general election for the vacancy is reasonably expected within 30 days or fewer, in which case the Chief Electoral Officer may exercise his or her discretion.
201.	When the Governor is absent, temporarily or otherwise, the individuals provided below may act as interim Governor with all the powers and duties as Governor until the Governor is no longer absent or until a new Governor duly enters office, in the order as follows: 
- the Lieutenant Governor, 
- the Speaker pro tempore,
- the Attorney General, and then
- a member of the Assembly, in order of seniority. 

202.	Generally, all acts of an interim official have the same effect as if done by the official in whose place the interim official is acting.
203.	If any elected official is absent, temporarily or otherwise, or has an interim official acting in place of that official for 30 calendar days or more in any 90-day period, that official is deemed to have resigned and his or her office is deemed to be vacated. 

## EMERGENCIES

204.	An emergency means an urgent and critical situation of a temporary nature that:
- poses a danger to the lives, health, or safety of the public and is of such proportions or nature as to exceed the capacity or authority of any other law of the State of Millbrooke to deal with the situation; or 
- seriously threatens the ability of the government to preserve the sovereignty, security, or territorial integrity of the state and that cannot be effectively dealt with under any other law of the State of Millbrooke.

205.	The Governor may, if he or she believes an emergency exists, issue a Declaration of a State of Emergency.
206.	A Declaration of a State of Emergency must specify
- concisely the state of affairs constituting the emergency; 
- the special temporary measures that the Governor anticipates may be necessary for dealing with the emergency; and 
- the duration of the declaration.

207.	A Declaration of a State of Emergency is effective on the day it is issued and expires 3 calendar days after the date on which it is issued unless it is earlier revoked.
208.	While a Declaration of a State of Emergency is in effect, the Governor may make such orders or regulations as the Governor believes, on reasonable grounds, are necessary for dealing with the emergency, including:
-  the regulation or prohibition of travel to, from or within any specified area, where necessary for the protection of the health or safety of individuals; 
- the requisition, use or disposition of property; 
- the regulation or prohibition of any public assembly that may reasonably be expected to lead to a breach of the peace; 
- the designation and securing of protected places; 
- enacting temporary emergency legislation; and 
- imposing penalties for contravention of any order or regulation made under emergency authority.

209.	No order or regulation may be issued under emergency authority as to:
- inhibit sittings of the Assembly, 
- inhibit the exercise of judicial review, or 
- require persons not employed by the state to serve a public function.

210.	Any orders and regulations issued under emergency authority is effective for the same period of time as the Declaration of a State of Emergency is in effect.
211.	The Governor must revoke a Declaration of a State of Emergency if he or she believes that the state of affairs constituting the emergency no longer constitutes an emergency.
212.	A Declaration of a State of Emergency may be revoked by the Assembly by resolution adopted on more than one-half of the whole of the membership of the Assembly voting in the affirmative.
213.	If, in the 30 calendar days following the date a Declaration of a State of Emergency was issued, the Governor wishes to make another Declaration of a State of Emergency, whether for the same or a different emergency, he or she must apply to the Supreme Court and justify such a declaration.

## DEVELOPERS

#### Developer defined

214.	A Developer means a developer of [Saturn Interactive]( https://www.roblox.com/communities/16811396/Saturn-lnteractive#!/about).

#### Special powers and privileges

215.	The Developers has all the powers and privileges of the Assembly and those granted to it in the Constitution and by law, but may not exercise any of them except:
- for inaugural appointments and enactments;
- for the purposes of inaugurating a department, agency, or other office;
- in cases where an emergency, conflict, or other situation exists which cannot be effectively dealt with under any existing law or by any existing legislative or judicial process; or
- where, in the opinion of the Developers, the emergency, conflict, or other situation endangers the viability of the State of Millbrooke. 

216.	The Developers may adopt bills and prescribe to the bill any of the following endorsements and restrictions:
- that the bill may not be amended by the Assembly;
- that the bill may not be repealed by the Assembly;
- that the bill does not require the Governor’s agreement before becoming law; and
- that the bill has supralegislative status and that any law adopted by the Assembly that is inconsistent with the provisions of the bill is, to the extent of the inconsistency, of no force or effect.

217.	The Developers may appoint a person to any vacant position or office.
218.	The Developers may remove any person occupying a position or office with immediate effect.
219.	The Developers may amend the Constitution unilaterally.
220.	The Developers may give directions to the Chief Electoral Officer respecting the exercise of his or her functions.
221.	The Developers may resolve that a judgment of the District Court or Supreme Court is unconscionable and set aside that judgment or order that an appeal be allowed in respect of that judgment.

#### Housing Authority

222.	The Housing Authority exclusively may grant or revoke a person's residency, on the basis of requirements and qualifications determined by the Developers.
223.	The Housing Authority is managed by the Developers.
224.	The Developers may appoint a Housing Director to be responsible for the duties of the Housing Authority.
225.	Administrative decisions of the Housing Authority are not subject to appeal or judicial review except in a manner as the Housing Authority may determine.
226.	The Housing Authority may enact binding Housing Regulations to the extent necessary to exercise its duties and powers. 
227.	The Housing Regulations retain supralegislative status, and any law which is in any respect repugnant to the Housing Regulations is of no force of effect.
228.	No law may require the Housing Authority to do or refrain from doing anything or otherwise regulate it.

#### Tollway Authority

229.	The Tollway Authority exclusively may construct, operate, and maintain tolled roads, tunnels, and bridges.
230.	The Tollway Authority is managed by the Developers.
231.	No law may require the Tollway Authority to do or refrain from doing anything or otherwise regulate it.
